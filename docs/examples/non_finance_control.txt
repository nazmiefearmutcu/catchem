Conservationists in Borneo released eight rehabilitated orangutans into
a protected stretch of secondary forest on Sunday, marking the largest
single release by the foundation in nearly a decade. The animals had
spent between four and nine years at a sanctuary outside Sandakan,
where they were raised after being rescued from logging concessions
and the illegal pet trade.

Researchers fitted each orangutan with a non-invasive radio collar
and will track movements over the coming months. Early data from
previous releases suggests rehabilitated juveniles tend to range
widely in the first weeks before settling into smaller home ranges
overlapping with existing wild groups.

A spokesperson for the foundation said the release site was chosen for
its low human-wildlife conflict history and abundant fruiting trees.
The forest sits inside a community-managed conservation area expanded
last year after a coalition of villages signed onto a long-term
stewardship agreement.

The foundation is preparing two more releases this season and continues
to operate veterinary care and behavioural-rehab programs for roughly
sixty orangutans still in its care.
